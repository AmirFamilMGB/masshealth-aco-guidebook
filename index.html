§ 01 · Program Evolution

A decade of iteration.

The MassHealth ACO program didn't arrive whole. It was built — and is still being rebuilt — through successive waivers, model versions, and methodology refinements. Here is the road that produced today's SDH 4.1 risk-adjusted payment formula.

2010FOUNDATION
DxCG Adoption
MassHealth begins risk-adjusting MCO capitation
Diagnosis-based Hierarchical Condition Category relative risk scores (DxCG-HCC RRS) become the core medical-acuity engine for Medicaid managed care rate-setting.
2016MILESTONE
SDH Model v1.0
Social determinants enter the formula
Arlene Ash and UMass Chan researchers add housing instability, disability flags, SMI/SUD, and the seven-variable Neighborhood Stress Score (NSS7) to the DxCG base. Effective October 2016 for managed care rates — the first state in the country to do this at scale.
2018LAUNCH
ACO Program — Wave 1
March 1: the statewide Medicaid ACO program goes live
Under the 1115 DSRIP waiver, MassHealth launches three ACO model types — Accountable Care Partnership Plans (ACPPs), Primary Care ACOs (PCACOs), and MCO-Administered ACOs. The SDH-augmented DxCG model risk-adjusts both capitation rates and TCOC benchmarks.
2020REFINEMENT
SDH Model v2.0
NSS becomes NSS+, RxCG joins the model
UMass implements a major restructuring: NSS becomes one-sided (only adds dollars in high-stress neighborhoods, never subtracts), pharmacy-based RxCG scores join the diagnosis-based DxCG, a housing × DxCG interaction is added, and the top-coding threshold rises from $125K to $200K.
2024PRODUCTION MODEL
SDH 4.0 + Area-Based Index Adjustment
NSS is replaced by the SVI-based Area-Based Index
MassHealth's production model becomes SDH 4.0, trained on CY19 data. As of January 2024, the Neighborhood Stress Score is retired entirely; in its place, an Area-Based Index (ABI) adjustment based on the CDC's Social Vulnerability Index is applied to non-medical rates only. Budget neutrality is calculated separately for core medical and non-medical components.
2023RELAUNCH
ACO 2.0
April: a refreshed five-year contract cycle
MassHealth's revamped ACO program begins with 17 ACOs under a new 1115 waiver (effective through December 2027). The Community Partners (CP) program is restructured, HRSN services formally enter the benefit, and the Primary Care Sub-Capitation Program launches in parallel.
2024PARALLEL TRACK
Primary Care Sub-Cap Refinement
Independent practice rate boost and clinical tier maturation
MassHealth increases Primary Care sub-cap rates for independent physicians and group practices. Clinical Tier 1/2/3 enhanced payments stabilize: $4.16–$13.52 PMPM. The Primary Care AcuLity (PCAL) model, co-developed with Randall Ellis, is approved for MassHealth ACOs.
2025METHODOLOGY SHIFT
Sub-Cap Decoupled from FFS
Population-based rates replace TIN-specific historical FFS rates
MassHealth decouples primary care sub-cap rates from each practice's own historical FFS experience. Rates are now calculated at an aggregate statewide level by provider type and patient mix, adjusted by the new "Primary Care Effort Model" — a primary-care-specific risk adjuster.
2026CURRENT MODEL
SDH 4.1 + ABA/SUD Integration
January 1: the SDH 4.1 model takes effect
MassHealth recalibrates from SDH 4.0 to 4.1 using SFY24 base data. ABA and SUD corridor services formally enter core medical and are trained into the model. New variables include ASD × age interactions (ages 3–5 and 6–20) and an OUD + SUD interactive term. Disability status splits into adult/child variants. Housing instability is dropped (only homelessness remains). DxCG v6.3 Model 88 and RxCG v6.3 Model 86 are the underlying engines. QEIP enters its disparities-reduction-payable phase in parallel.
2027HORIZON
Waiver Renewal Window
The 1115 demonstration expires December 31
The current Section 1115 waiver — under which the ACO program operates — runs through year-end 2027. Renegotiation with CMS will shape ACO 3.0.
§ 02 · Payment Cycle

When the money actually moves.

A capitation payment for January 2026 isn't priced using January 2026 data — it's priced using claims that ran out months earlier, with a snapshot taken in October 2025, and won't be truly settled until late 2027. Three different "risk score" calculations happen in three different windows. Here's the full cycle, dates and all.

Risk-adjusted Medicaid payments are like a slow-motion three-act play. The prospective score sets the rate before the year begins. Concurrent estimates track performance through the year but don't change payment. The final concurrent calculation — run months after the year ends — is what truly settles the books.

Act One

Prospective

Calculated before the rate year begins, using a base period that ended months earlier. Sets the actual PMPM the ACPP receives each month of the rate year.

Purpose: set payment.

For RY26 effective 1/1/26:
Base period: Mar 1, 2024 – Feb 28, 2025
Snapshot: Oct 1, 2025
Act Two

Concurrent Estimates

Run every 4–6 months during the rate year using performance-year data. Members are weighted by their actual time enrolled, not a single snapshot.

Purpose: tracking only — does not change revenue.

For RY26:
Estimate runs: ~Q2, Q3, Q4 2026
Method: performance-year weighted
Act Three

Final Concurrent

The definitive recalculation run 9–12 months after the rate year closes. This replaces the prospective score in reconciliation, truing-up plan revenue.

Purpose: reconcile and settle.

For RY26:
Calculation: ~Oct 2027 – Dec 2027
Used in: ACPP/MCO recon, PCACO shared savings/losses

The dates that matter.

These are the dates an ACPP, MCO, or PCACO actually has on its calendar for the RY26 cycle. Service dates that fall inside the base period are what feed the model; service dates inside the rate year are what determine the final concurrent settlement.

Jul 1, 2023 – Jun 30, 2024
Reference Risk Period

The "baseline acuity" period Mercer uses to calculate reference risk scores — providing a normalization anchor that captures rate-setting assumptions.

Mar 1, 2024 – Feb 28, 2025
SDH 4.1 Base Period

The 12 months of MassHealth claims and encounters that get scored through the SDH 4.1 model to produce each member's raw risk score for RY26.

Oct 1, 2025
Enrollment Snapshot

A point-in-time freeze that determines which plan each member is attributed to, what region they're in, and what rate cell they fall into for the prospective rate.

Jan 1, 2026
Rate Year Begins

Prospective PMPMs (medical, non-medical, ABI adjustment, payor surcharge, HCD add-on) start flowing monthly to plans.

Apr / Aug / Dec 2026
Concurrent Estimates

Three informational risk-score runs during the rate year. Plans use them to forecast where they'll land. Revenue does not change.

Dec 31, 2026
Rate Year Closes

The 12-month performance period ends. Claims for RY26 service dates continue to develop for ~6+ months as providers bill out.

Oct – Dec 2027
Final Concurrent Run

Approximately 9–12 months after the rate year closes, MassHealth runs the final concurrent score on actual RY26 performance data with claims runout complete.

Late 2027 / Early 2028
Reconciliation

The final concurrent replaces the prospective score in payment math. ACPPs and MCOs receive (or owe) the true-up. PCACOs' shared savings/losses are calculated.

Which service dates drive which payments?

A common point of confusion: the claims used to calculate a payment month's risk score are not from that payment month — they're from a window that closed long before. Here's the mapping.

Service Date Window Role in Payment Which Rate Year? When does it affect dollars?
Jul 2023 – Jun 2024 Reference risk normalization anchor RY26 Used to set prospective RY26 rates
Mar 2024 – Feb 2025 SDH 4.1 model scoring input RY26 Drives prospective RY26 PMPM
Jan 2026 – Dec 2026 Performance year for RY26 RY26 Drives final concurrent in Oct–Dec 2027 → RY26 reconciliation
Mar 2025 – Feb 2026 SDH 4.x model scoring input RY27 Will drive prospective RY27 PMPM (rates set late 2026)
Jan 2027 – Dec 2027 Performance year for RY27 RY27 Will drive RY27 reconciliation in late 2028
Why this timing matters operationally

A diagnosis captured on a claim with service date March 2024 will affect an ACPP's revenue for every month of calendar year 2026 — almost two years later. Coding done in 2024 is still moving 2026 dollars. Conversely, a diagnosis captured in February 2025 is in the base period; a diagnosis from March 2025 is one day too late and waits a full year to count. This is why MassHealth ACPPs invest so heavily in retrospective chart review and prospective documentation programs: the marginal value of a captured diagnosis isn't immediate, but it compounds across two payment years.

One more nuance worth knowing. Concurrent estimates during the rate year are calculated using the performance year as their base period — with each member's contribution weighted by their actual time enrolled, not a single snapshot. This is structurally different from the prospective score, which uses a fixed October snapshot. That's why a plan's concurrent estimate can drift meaningfully from its prospective score even when nothing material has changed — the methodology itself is different.

Putting it together: one member's year.

The abstract concepts of prospective / concurrent / final concurrent come into focus when traced through a single member over time. Walking through the year, quarter by quarter.

Q1

January – March 2026 · Baseline

Daniel enters RY26 with the prospective score MassHealth locked in October 2025. That score was built from his March 2024 – February 2025 claims — when his coded conditions were just HTN and hyperlipidemia. His individual raw RRS is 0.62. The plan-cell normalized score for RC I Adult Greater Boston is 1.014. The ACPP receives $755.80 PMPM for Daniel every month, regardless of what happens to him during the year. Q1 monthly claims: ~$300 total (one PCP visit, two pharmacy fills).

Q2

April – June 2026 · The first event

April 14: Daniel presents to the ED with chest pain. STEMI. Activated cath lab, three stents, three-day ICU stay. April claims spike to ~$93K. The ACPP's monthly payment for Daniel does not change. MassHealth's Q2 concurrent estimate (run around April – June using performance-year claims with runout) begins to pick up the cardiac diagnoses, but six-month runout means Daniel's April events barely show in the Q2 numbers yet. The Q2 estimate puts his individual score at ~0.7 — still close to baseline. May and June: cardiac rehab, multiple medications, follow-up visits. Q2 claims: ~$115K cumulative.

Q3

July – September 2026 · Catastrophic

July 21: sudden cardiac arrest at home. EMS resuscitation, prolonged downtime, anoxic brain injury. Six-week ICU stay. Tracheostomy. Multiple ventilator weaning attempts. Cumulative claims jump to ~$370K by end of July. The August concurrent estimate is now picking up the April cardiac events more fully — Daniel's individual estimated score rises to ~3.8. Plan-cell score barely moves; he's one member in a cell of thousands. The ACPP's monthly payment for him remains $755.80. September: stable but ventilator-dependent, transferred to LTAC. Q3 claims cumulative: ~$680K.

Q4

October – December 2026 · Complications and year-end

Ongoing LTAC stay, multiple infections, dialysis initiation for AKI converted to chronic. Two readmissions to acute care. December concurrent estimate puts Daniel's individual score at ~8.9 — visible to the ACPP in member-level files but still not changing payment. By December 31, his year-to-date claims hit ~$1.4M. The ACPP has received $9,070 in capitation revenue for him over the full year. Reinsurance attachment was hit in late July; the ACPP's reinsurance carrier (commercial, contracted at $250K attach with 90% coinsurance) has been accumulating recoverable claims throughout Q3 and Q4.

'27

2027 · Runout, final concurrent, reconciliation

Daniel continues care into 2027 — that's RY27, separately scored and paid. Through Q1-Q3 2027, RY26 claims continue developing as providers finish billing. In late 2027 (October – December), MassHealth runs the final concurrent calculation on the RY26 performance year. Daniel's individual raw score in that calculation is ~10.4. The plan's RC I Adult Greater Boston score for RY26 moves from 1.014 (prospective) to approximately 1.016 (final concurrent) — a microscopic shift because Daniel is one member among thousands. Reconciliation in late 2027 / early 2028 trues up the difference: the plan receives an additional ~$3.50 PMPM × 12 months × all RC I Adult GB member-months. Daniel's contribution to that true-up is real but very small in dollar terms.

Separately, the ACPP files its reinsurance claim for Daniel. Reinsurer pays out $1.035M (90% of the $1.15M above attachment). Combined with the original capitation and the tiny reconciliation true-up, the ACPP's net loss on Daniel is approximately $356K.

The takeaway visible only in the temporal view

Risk adjustment — at every stage, prospective and concurrent and final — is a population-level mechanism that operates on plan-cell averages. It is mathematically incapable of pricing a single catastrophic event because individual extreme acuity barely moves a plan-cell mean. The temporal walkthrough makes this concrete: Daniel's individual estimated score rises from 0.62 to 10.4 over the year, but the plan-cell score he contributes to moves from 1.014 to ~1.016. Reinsurance, by contrast, operates at the member level and is the only mechanism in the entire payment system designed to handle catastrophic single-member events. This is why MassHealth requires every ACPP to carry it.

§ 03 · The Formula

What an ACO is actually paid.

Strip away the jargon and a MassHealth ACO's monthly payment is a stack of multiplicative and additive adjustments built on a per-member, per-month base. Here is the full structure.

The payment that flows from MassHealth to an Accountable Care Partnership Plan begins with a base capitation rate set for each rating category — a population segment defined by Medicaid eligibility characteristics. That base is then risk-adjusted at the member level, multiplied by member-months, adjusted for plan-wide factors, and reconciled at year-end through risk corridors, reinsurance, and quality incentives.

// THE RY26 ACPP MONTHLY PAYMENT, IN ESSENCE PMPMACPP = Medical Capitation + Non-Medical Capitation + HCD Add-on
  Medical = Base Medicalcell, region × Normalized Risk Score
  Non-Medical = ½(Base Non-Medical) + ½(Base Non-Medical × Norm. Risk Score)            + Area-Based Index Adjustment (SVI)            + Payor Surcharge PMPM
  HCD Add-on = flat amount, not risk-adjusted
  Then reconcile annually: risk corridors · QEIP earn-back · reinsurance recoveries

§ 04 · Risk Adjustment

Inside the SDH 4.1 risk score.

The risk score is what makes ACOs financially indifferent between healthy and complex members. Effective January 1, 2026, MassHealth uses the SDH 4.1 model — a concurrent risk-adjustment model built on diagnoses, pharmacy, demographics, and social-risk markers, recalibrated on SFY24 data.

The headline engine is DxCG v6.3 Model #88 — Cotiviti's diagnosis-based Hierarchical Condition Category model, which converts a member's diagnoses into a relative risk score where 1.0 represents the population-average member. SDH 4.1 layers on a UMass-developed overlay built from RxCG v6.3 Model #86 (pharmacy acuity), demographic age-sex cells, hierarchical disability (split adult/child), adult- and child-specific behavioral health hierarchies, ASD interactions by age, opioid use disorder with or without co-occurring SUD, homelessness, rurality, DCF involvement, HIV, Type 1 diabetes, and maternal/newborn complexity.

Two important things SDH 4.1 does not do. It no longer includes the Neighborhood Stress Score — the neighborhood-level social-vulnerability signal moved out of the model entirely in January 2024 and is now handled separately as the Area-Based Index adjustment applied to non-medical rates (see §05). And housing instability (the "3+ addresses" proxy) was groomed out in the 4.0→4.1 update — only ICD-coded homelessness (Z59.0X) remains.

Validation: the SDH 4.1 model fits its population

MassHealth validated SDH 4.1 against a SFY24 holdout sample of 337,956 members. Across nearly every subpopulation — DMH clients, ASD children, homeless members, all behavioral-health tiers, age-sex cells — the observed-to-expected cost ratios sit between 0.97 and 1.05. The average annual cost across the full 1.1M-member groomed pool is $7,401 observed vs $7,407 predicted (O:E = 1.00). The model isn't perfect, but it's calibrated.

§ 05 · Other Adjusters

Beyond the risk score: everything else that moves the dollars.

The risk score is the marquee piece, but a half-dozen other levers reshape payment between gross capitation and what an ACO actually keeps at year-end. Each one is a deliberate policy choice with budget consequences.

  • A
    Rate Cells × Regions

    Each member is assigned to one of six rating categories (RC I Adult, RC I Child, RC II Adult, RC II Child, RC IX Adult, RC X Adult) within one of five regions (Northern, Greater Boston, Southern, Central, Western) — 26 distinct rate cells, each with its own base medical and non-medical PMPM.

  • B
    Area-Based Index Adjustment (SVI)

    Replaced NSS in Jan 2024. Applied prospectively and annually to non-medical rates only. Members in the top 20% of CDC's 2022 Social Vulnerability Index receive +$10 PMPM; bottom 50% receive −$4.06 PMPM. Budget-neutral redistribution across MCO/ACPP/PCACO enrollees.

  • C
    HCD (High-Cost Drug) Add-on

    A flat PMPM amount added to ACPP and MCO capitation to fund a designated list of high-cost specialty drugs (hemophilia factors, certain gene therapies, etc.). Not risk-adjusted. Reconciled separately for actual utilization.

  • D
    Payor Surcharge PMPM

    A flat PMPM amount added to the non-medical rate covering state-mandated surcharges. Varies by rate cell and region (typically $2–$10 PMPM).

  • E
    Primary Care Sub-Capitation

    ACPPs are required to direct primary care payment through a sub-cap to PCP TINs. Since 2025, sub-cap rates are set at the population level using age, disability status, and the Primary Care Effort Model — not each practice's own FFS history.

  • F
    Reinsurance / Stop-Loss

    MassHealth requires every MCO/ACPP to carry commercial reinsurance against catastrophic single-member costs. Typical attachment points are $250K–$500K with 80–90% coinsurance; per-member ceilings range from $2M to unlimited.

  • G
    Risk Corridors

    Symmetric aggregate-level sharing between ACPP and the state. Limits both upside and downside; ACPPs typically retain full risk in an inner band, share outside it, and the state absorbs extreme outliers. Triggers on the entire book, not individual members.

  • H
    Quality & Equity Incentive Program (QEIP)

    Up to ~0.75% of capitation withheld and earned back based on quality and disparities-reduction performance. MassHealth has contractual authority to expand this to ~1.5%. In 2026, payment moves from reporting to actually reducing disparities.

Why the layering matters

Two ACOs with identical capitation revenue can have very different bottom lines after these adjusters resolve. An ACO that under-codes its members loses risk-score lift; an ACO that misses QEIP targets forfeits the withhold; an ACO with an unusually high-cost member can have most of that loss absorbed by reinsurance. The capitation rate is the starting line, not the finish.

02

Normalize at the plan-rate-cell level

Critically, MassHealth doesn't pay on Maria's individual score. Individual raw scores roll up to a plan-rate-cell average (e.g., "Plan X's average raw score across all RC II Adult members in Greater Boston"). That plan-cell average is then normalized two ways: against a Mercer-calculated reference risk score (capturing rate-setting assumptions) and against a budget-neutrality factor so the entire managed care market averages to 1.0.

What this teaches the market

The four program-level adjusters didn't rescue this ACPP. In this particular year, they actually moved the bottom line from a gross underwriting loss of $24.3M to a net loss of $37.5M — because reinsurance premiums exceeded recoveries (the cost of carrying coverage in a moderate-loss year), the corridor providing only modest relief, and QEIP performance leaving 40% of the withhold unrecovered. In a good year, the same structure works in reverse: reinsurance premium gets absorbed into a year of low recoveries, the corridor claws back gains above the inner band, and full QEIP earn-back means no forfeit at all. The mechanics are symmetric by design — they smooth volatility, they don't subsidize losses. An ACPP that can't operate profitably at near-average risk over a multi-year horizon won't be rescued by these tools.

§ 08 · Under the Hood

A reader's guide to the model — its parts, its limits, its quiet assumptions.

We've covered how the formula works. This chapter goes deeper into what each piece of the SDH 4.1 model is actually made of: which engine is commercial-trained, which layer is state-tailored, where the model's quiet blind spots live, and how to fact-check claims about how it behaves on specific populations. By the end you should be able to read any future MassHealth rate-year methodology and parse what's commercial-engine inheritance versus what's MassHealth-specific overlay.

When MassHealth says "Maria's plan score for RC II Adult Greater Boston is 1.069," that number is the output of three nested calculations: a commercial-trained engine (DxCG and RxCG), a state-tailored overlay (SDH 4.1's coefficient table), and a normalization step that converts predicted dollars to a relative score. Each layer has its own training data, its own intended use, and its own limitations. Understanding which layer is doing what work is how you read the model.

A model built in two layers.

MassHealth doesn't build its risk model from scratch. It rents an engine and then tailors it. The "engine" is Cotiviti's commercial DxCG/RxCG product line. The "tailor" is the SDH overlay — coefficients developed by UMass Chan and EOHHS using Massachusetts-specific data to better predict cost in the actual MassHealth population. Both layers feed into the final dollar prediction; pulling them apart is how you understand what's doing what.

Figure 8.1 · The model stack — how inputs become a final plan score

Read top to bottom: a final plan score of 1.069 starts from raw claims and eligibility data, gets converted to dollar predictions by two commercial-trained engines, gets adjusted by a Massachusetts-specific overlay, aggregated to the plan-cell level, and normalized against a reference. The commercial engines do the heavy mathematical lifting — they're sophisticated models with hundreds of internal condition categories — but they were trained on a different population than MassHealth's. The overlay is what bridges that gap, partially.

What "commercial-trained" actually means.

When the SDH 4.1 documentation describes DxCG #88 and RxCG #86 as "developed using commercial insurance data," it's not metadata trivia. It's the most consequential design fact about the engine MassHealth runs on top of. To understand why, think about what training a regression model actually does: it learns the relationship between an input (a diagnosis, a pharmacy claim) and an output (predicted cost) by fitting coefficients against the empirical relationships in the training data.

If your training data is commercially-insured working adults and their dependents — a population with employer coverage, generally stable housing, continuous insurance, employer-sponsored access to care — then the coefficient your model learns for, say, "moderate persistent asthma in a 7-year-old" reflects what that diagnosis costs in that population. Children whose parents have commercial coverage get scheduled pediatric visits, fill inhaler prescriptions reliably, and don't generally end up in the ED at 2 AM with status asthmaticus.

Run the same model on a Medicaid pediatric population — a 7-year-old with the same coded asthma diagnosis but living in older housing with environmental triggers, with parents working multiple shift jobs and less access to scheduled specialist follow-up — and the diagnosis predicts a different cost trajectory. The diagnosis is the same. The coefficient is the same. The actual cost is different. That's the calibration gap.

Figure 8.2 · The calibration gap, illustrated

Two clarifications about how this mechanism actually operates inside the commercial engine are worth making explicit, because they shape what's possible to fix downstream.

First, the commercial engines take diagnoses and pharmacy claims as inputs — not socioeconomic variables. The SDH 4.1 documentation describes Model #88 as predicting cost "from diagnoses." It does not ingest parental employment status, household income, or housing stability as direct predictors. Social context isn't a feature the engine reads in for each member; it's an empirical regularity of the training population that gets baked into the dollar weights the model learns for each diagnosis category.

Second, that distinction has a practical consequence: the calibration gap cannot be closed by adding new variables to the existing commercial engine. The engine has no "unemployed parent" coefficient that could be retuned for Medicaid pediatrics. The available lever is a separate overlay that adds Massachusetts-specific dollar contributions on top of the diagnosis-based engine output — coefficients fit against MassHealth data on conditions and statuses that the state can observe directly: DDS enrollment, DCF involvement, the behavioral health hierarchy, homelessness ICD codes, ASD by age, OUD with or without co-occurring SUD. That overlay is what SDH 4.1 contributes. It is, by design, the bridge between a commercial-trained engine and a Medicaid deployment population.

For pediatrics specifically, the overlay is small but heavily weighted — ten variables that fire on specific eligibility flags or diagnosis groupings, each compensating for a known pattern of commercial-engine under-prediction.

What the pediatric overlay catches.

SDH 4.1 layers ten specifically-pediatric variables on top of DxCG #88. Five of them are very high-value coefficients; the rest are calibration adjustments. Together they compensate for the commercial-engine gap in the populations where it matters most — but they only fire when their triggering condition is actually captured.

What the overlay still doesn't catch.

Look carefully at the variable list. Every pediatric-specific coefficient depends on a categorical trigger — a flag that's either present or absent. A child either is or isn't a DDS client. They either have or haven't been coded with F84.x for autism. They either have or haven't crossed into the SED diagnostic group. The overlay catches kids who fall into those buckets. It does not catch kids whose Medicaid-driven cost trajectory exceeds the commercial calibration without falling into one of those buckets.

The canonical missed case: the pediatric Medicaid asthmatic with no DDS, no DCF, no SED diagnosis — just a moderate persistent asthma diagnosis and a Medicaid card. For that child, the model relies entirely on DxCG #88's commercial-trained coefficient for that asthma category. If the commercial coefficient says "moderate persistent asthma in this age band predicts $1,800/year of cost," that's what the model assigns. If the actual Medicaid-population cost is $2,800/year (because of more ED visits and worse environmental control), the kid is under-priced by ~$1,000.

Devonte is the case that tests the model. DxCG #88 will assign him a moderate-asthma coefficient based on the commercial training distribution. The SDH 4.1 overlay will add no pediatric-specific dollars because no overlay trigger fires — he's not DDS, not DCF, not in any BH bucket. The ABI adjustment will apply if he lives in a top-20% SVI tract, but ABI is non-medical only and small ($10 PMPM). The bulk of his pricing is the commercial DxCG coefficient. If his actual cost trajectory exceeds the commercial-training expectation, the model under-prices him — and his ACPP absorbs the difference.

This isn't a bug. It's a known limitation of the architectural choice to use a commercial engine plus a categorical overlay. The fix would be to recalibrate DxCG itself on Medicaid pediatric data — which would require Cotiviti to maintain a Medicaid-trained model variant, or for MassHealth to build a pediatric-specific overlay variable that fires on every Medicaid-enrolled child with a chronic condition (not just the DDS/DCF/BH subset). Neither has happened yet.

Why "O:E = 1.02 for Male 7–12" doesn't fully answer the calibration question

Aggregate O:E ratios near 1.0 are reassuring but they hide within-group distributional fit. A model can have O:E = 1.00 on "Male 7–12" by over-predicting cost for the easy 90% of that group and under-predicting for the high-complexity 10%, with the errors netting to zero in the aggregate. The MassHealth-published O:E table shows group means — it doesn't show whether the model fits the complex tail of each pediatric subpopulation. That's the question Devonte and others like him would actually answer. A more rigorous diagnostic would be O:E ratios within the top decile of predicted cost for each pediatric age band, which is not provided in the public documentation.

What this means operationally.

If you're an ACPP financial officer reading this, three implications matter most.

One: coding completeness is asymmetrically valuable for populations where the engine under-prices. For Devonte-type kids — Medicaid pediatric with chronic conditions but no overlay-triggering flags — getting every diagnosis captured matters more than for an adult Maria-type member who already has DMH, homelessness Z-code, and an OUD interaction filling out her score. The marginal documented diagnosis lifts her score by a known amount; for Devonte, it's the only lever the model offers.

Two: risk corridors are the backstop. MassHealth's aggregate risk-sharing arrangement provides protection at the plan-book level. If an ACPP's overall experience deviates significantly from prospective capitation, corridors kick in. This is the backstop against systematic mis-pricing of populations like the asthmatic-Medicaid-kid cohort. It doesn't fix the individual under-pricing, but it caps the aggregate plan-level exposure.

Three: serving complex Medicaid pediatric populations is a strategic decision. Children's hospitals and pediatric-heavy networks know this empirically: their populations have higher actual cost than commercial-pediatric calibration predicts, even after SDH 4.1 overlay adjustments. They negotiate carve-outs, special contractual terms, or supplemental payment arrangements when possible. They also lobby MassHealth for overlay refinements that would catch their populations more accurately. The 4.0→4.1 changes (adding ASD × age, breaking out OUD/SUD) came from exactly this kind of pressure.

Where the model is going.

The SDH model has changed substantially over its lifecycle, and the trajectory tells you something about what's likely next. The 2016 launch was DxCG plus a relatively simple SDH overlay focused on disability, behavioral health, and the original Neighborhood Stress Score. The 2024 update (SDH 4.0) retired NSS entirely in favor of the Area-Based Index on non-medical rates, broke out housing and homelessness, and standardized variable definitions. The 2026 update (SDH 4.1) added two interaction terms (ASD × age and OUD/SUD), removed the low-predictive housing instability variable, split disability adult/child, and recalibrated on more recent SFY24 data.

The pattern: each update both adds variables where new clinical evidence justifies them (ASD × age came from ABA services entering core medical) and removes variables that turn out to add little predictive value beyond what DxCG already captures (housing instability, low-complexity newborns). The model is converging toward fewer but better-targeted overlay variables, with the underlying commercial engine doing more of the lifting and the overlay handling the specifically-Medicaid carve-outs.

For future rate years — RY27, RY28, and the likely SDH 4.x or 5.0 successor — three trajectories appear most plausible. First, more interaction terms: combinations of conditions that interact non-linearly tend to be where prediction error concentrates. Second, possibly a Medicaid-specific recalibration of the commercial engines (if Cotiviti releases a Medicaid-trained DxCG variant, MassHealth would likely adopt). Third, continued tension between expanding the overlay (which improves fit but adds complexity) and trusting DxCG splines to handle high-acuity tails (which keeps the model parsimonious).

What's unlikely to change: the two-layer architecture itself. Commercial engines like DxCG and RxCG are products with hundreds of person-years of actuarial development invested in them. No state would (or should) rebuild that from scratch. The interesting question is always how thick the overlay layer needs to be to bridge the calibration gap, and SDH 4.1 represents MassHealth's current best answer.

A user's manual for the model, in one paragraph

For Rate Year 2026, MassHealth's risk-adjusted PMPM is built by feeding a member's diagnoses through DxCG v6.3 Model #88 (commercial-trained) and their pharmacy claims through RxCG v6.3 Model #86 (commercial-trained), then adding dollar contributions from the SDH 4.1 overlay (Massachusetts-trained on SFY24 data) for variables like disability category, behavioral health hierarchy, ASD × age, OUD/SUD, homelessness, rurality, DCF involvement, HIV, T1D, maternal delivery, and newborn complexity. The member's individual predicted cost gets aggregated to the plan-rate-cell level, normalized against a Mercer-supplied reference score and a budget-neutrality factor, and produces a single plan score for that cell. The plan score multiplies the cell's base medical PMPM, the half-methodology applies to the non-medical PMPM, an Area-Based Index adjustment derived from CDC's 2022 SVI adjusts non-medical further, a payor surcharge is added, and an HCD add-on (not risk-adjusted) lands on top. The whole calculation is run prospectively in October 2025 for January 2026 payment, refreshed concurrently every 4–6 months during the rate year, and reconciled finally in late 2027. That's the model.
